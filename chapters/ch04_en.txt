From the Simplicity of Materialism to the Spaciousness of Humanism and Faith. The Erosion of the Material Model. Perhaps the pivotal existential and intellectual experience in my life is the dominance of the materialist philosophical model over me for some time (after doubt swept over me in Damanhour), then my gradual realization of the futility of materialist analytical models in encompassing the complex human phenomenon (given the simplicity, naivety, and reductionism of these models), and my growing feeling of the necessity of adopting complex analytical models, multi-dimensional and multi-leveled, if one wished to capture the humanity of the human being (not his materiality or his material nature) and to see him in all his complexity. For the human being is the most honored of creatures in the universe, fundamentally different from the rest of beings even if he shares some of their characteristics. He lives in nature but is separate from it. (I later developed the concept of nature/matter, for I hold that the attributes of nature in most Western philosophical discourse are the very attributes of matter in the philosophical sense. And so I see that wherever nature appears, one should understand it as nature/matter. I also developed the concept of the distance that separates the human being from nature, and the Creator from the created, and the body from the spirit -- which means that there is a fundamental duality in the universe, and that the universe is diverse, plural, non-homogeneous, containing the absolute and the relative, the fixed and the changing; they may clash, they may meet, they may interact, but they are different. All of this stands at the antipode of material monism, which holds that the entire world -- human beings and nature -- is of a single substance.) The world (human beings and nature), for me, is characterized by what I call "loose duality." Loose duality is a term that stands opposite to monism. Duality is the belief in the existence of more than one substance in the world.

And naturally, my belief in material rationality made me no exception to this rule. Adopting the materialist model meant in reality adopting the Western model (the Marxist one, in my case). The vast distance that separates what the model preaches (its ideals in which I believe) from reality... I used to imagine, like many others, that Western civilization is the civilization of individualism and that our civilization is the Eastern collective civilization. This is what we were taught, and this is how we perceived the universe (and of course there were the ready-made scientific theses that explained this: capitalist economy, Enlightenment thought, Western Christianity... etc.). But when I went there, I noticed that there was an astonishing uniformity in forms of life and in human patterns -- a matter that Western sociology had documented, especially after the emergence of specialized sciences of controlling human behavior, whether at work or in private life, which rationalized and regulated the person's life according to a specific plan (sleep, breakfast, work) so that everything became pre-arranged, even vacations and celebrations and indeed funerals, all arranged, organized, and planned. There now exists the occupation of "wedding director" (an occupation that has begun to appear in our countries as well), who organizes everything for you, and the person concerned cannot change anything. My first contact with the extreme uniformity that characterizes life in the United States occurred suddenly. In the mid-1960s, I took a bus trip across the United States (from New York to Minnesota) that lasted two days. The bus would stop at stations with branches of Howard Johnson restaurants. We would get off, and the waitresses would come, smile, and serve us the food we ordered. I ate the food with appetite the first time and thanked them for the excellent service.

And fashion (meaning clothing), which has become one of the most important and largest industries, is the greatest proof of this. The declared purpose of changing fashions is to give the woman the opportunity to renew and change her clothes as she pleases, thus expressing her self. But if you looked closely at the matter, you would find that if every woman actually unleashed her imagination and expressed her individuality outside all limits, restrictions, and barriers, the women's clothing factories would stop running, because the woman's behavior would become unpredictable and the monopolies could not prepare their million-unit production lines! Here comes the task of fashion: it regulates (rationalizes) the woman's behavior by setting the basic lines within which she moves (the long green dress is the fashion this year, while next year it is the short blue one, and in the third year it is either this or that -- and spin me around, oh lemon!). And thereby her behavior can be predicted and she (and her dreams) can be absorbed within the production lines. Indeed, consumerism tries to determine for a person the purpose of his life -- that is, it places the human being and his family inside specific molds so that all inner aspects of his life are regulated through the dream of consumption. That is, if external rationalization objectifies him from the outside, internal rationalization objectifies him from the inside -- meaning it is a complete process of regulation. I believe that this is the backbone of the power of the United States, for it has succeeded in regulating the behavior of these millions and directing them toward a single goal: production and consumption, and made them internalize these ideals as an ultimate goal and source of meaning, and strive for them. I believe that foreign aid plays a parallel role for Third World countries, for they are countries that contain peoples of different ethnic and religious origins, and the individuals in them have multiple loyalties and different dreams: individual, familial, tribal, national, and religious.

And so the critical process for the critics of this school takes the form of an attempt to decode the text from within, through what is called "close reading" -- a critical reading that focuses on the internal relations of the text and excludes many historical, social, cultural, and psychological elements. They held that within every great work of art there exists an awareness of the paradox that characterizes human existence (some of them believed that the greatest paradox was the crucifixion of Christ and then his resurrection -- from his death, life is born; from his defeat, victory is born). They held that what distinguishes the human phenomenon from the natural phenomenon is the paradox, which the language of poetry is capable of expressing, for it can speak of the thing and its opposite at the same time, unlike the abstract language of science, which can only deal with abstract scientific laws and with the thing or its opposite. Hence poetry and metaphor become matters integral to human existence itself, and it is impossible to express feelings... I did not adopt the vision of the New Criticism thinkers regarding the literary text, but I was nonetheless deeply influenced by some of their critical and philosophical propositions -- such as their distinction between the scientific (natural, material) phenomenon and the human phenomenon, and their deep skepticism of science as a model insufficient for expressing what is human. I also always tried to see the literary text as an entity containing many complex elements, of which paradox may be one, but it is not necessarily the most important, and that the structure and form of the text parallel (without reflecting) the structure of the historical moment. And so I benefited greatly from the method of reading texts without adopting the model of hostility toward history latent behind it.

Then I added, saying: the presence of the cosmic element within the historical structure is the source of its renewal. The interplay between the cosmic and the historical is the foundation of progress and movement. For the individual human being exists within the historical circle and is absorbed in it, and if this absorption is complete and total, then the human being loses the desire for revolution. But because he is within the historical structure and at the same time in contact with cosmic, non-historical elements, he is not fully absorbed; rather, he retains the ability to withdraw within himself and to establish a direct connection with the universe. Through this process, he reformulates himself and acquires the foundations of life that make him dissatisfied with what surrounds him and instead propose new visions. Let us note that the cosmic element is the source of revolutionism if it remains interactive with the historical element. But if it becomes independent, then the human being becomes the individual human being of narrow limits. Yet at the same time, he is the cosmic human being whom no limits confine -- and this is the essence of capitalist polarization: the bourgeois human being goes to nature or to the market; he is a non-social individual, a world unto himself, completely closed, connected to others by no bond, yet a world that no limits confine. He merges with nature if he wishes, seizes surplus value without any restrictions, produces whatever goods he pleases, and sells them at the price he sees fit. But the same applies to the historical element: if the historical element does not interact with the cosmic element, the human being becomes the arid bureaucratic human being who has lost the dream, who is content with committee decisions and five-year and seven-year plans, who rejoices at the direction of authority and grieves when asked to do so -- and there is no power nor strength except in God.

The poems of William Wordsworth also had the deepest effect on my soul. In his poem titled "London, 1802," the poet attacks the utilitarian values that prevailed in his homeland. This material rationalist view of the human being strips him of sanctity and makes him lose his centrality in the universe, and this is what the philosophers of the dark Enlightenment realized. Perhaps Hobbes was the first thinker to put his finger on the dark propositions within material rationality (and so we speak of the "dark Enlightenment"), when he declared that the state of nature (the state of the human being after the withdrawal of God from the universe) is a state of the war of all against all, for man is a wolf to his fellow man. The social contract between human beings will be concluded not because of a good nature in them, but out of their extreme fear and their love of survival, so they set up the Leviathan state as ruler over them so they can achieve a degree, however small, of tranquility. Machiavelli agreed with him in this. As for Spinoza (and Newton), they presented a fully mechanical world in which the self dissolves in the mechanical movement of the universe. Locke showed that the mind is a blank slate on which data accumulates. Bentham showed that the morality of the human being is connected to his drives and instincts alone. The Marquis de Sade, Darwin, and Freud showed that the human being contains the wolf within him and outside him, and that this civilized self of his is nothing but a flimsy crust concealing a darkness churning inside the human being and around him. Jung showed that there is no individual self, but rather a collective self containing archetypes. Nietzsche crystallized the foundations of the dark Enlightenment when he showed that the self is one of the tricks by which the weak try to choke the innocence and spontaneity of power. The self is what imposes the illusory ideals of stable existence upon the world of becoming, and it is in reality merely a mask, an ornament, an ideological synthesis, a linguistic position called "the self" that has no real existence.
