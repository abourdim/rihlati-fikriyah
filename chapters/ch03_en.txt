In the United States, I developed the ritual of the intellectual bath. Whenever an idea proves difficult for me, I go to take a hot shower, and under the shower, ideas begin to coalesce and the relationships between them become clear, and I resolve the intellectual problem confronting me. (One of the doctors informed me that this last ritual has a material basis: I suffer from an allergy to the pollen grains that are widespread in the United States, and so when I take a hot shower, the rising steam cleanses the nasal sinuses, making breathing easier, and oxygen rises to my brain, so I think with greater freedom.) This ritualistic tendency is, in reality, a tendency to place boundaries between myself and the immediate material reality, and in this it resembles my awareness of history and art. It also evolved later into an inclination toward crystallizing analytical categories and perceiving the different levels of reality. This tendency increased in the United States, for it is a country that does not respect rituals and knows the barest minimum of them. The rituals of transition from one age stage to another are either nonexistent or different from what I was accustomed to; they are not sufficiently rich. Moreover, in most cases they take an obviously consumerist form (such as the celebrations of coming of age among Jews, or the celebrations of entering university or graduating from it). Perhaps to protect my self and surround it with a fence separating it from its surroundings, there was no escaping the establishment and care of rituals. But the most important element that aided my separation is what I call the malady of contemplation, which struck me one day in my childhood or the beginnings of youth (perhaps at the age of twelve), when I grasped the concept of time and that we live inside it, and that our life is time itself. And based on this, I set out from this concept. So I would -- to save time, and consequently to save my life -- ask one of the servants to bring me my shoes (for example).

I also brought translations of a number of the best plays and novels so that I could command the civilizational discourse of the West and so that my knowledge of Western literary traditions would deepen, just as I had commanded the language. (I underwent a unique experience that summer: I procured a translation of Emile Zola's novel Germinal and decided to read it without stopping, so that I could feel it as a complete organic whole. Indeed, I sat for three days and three nights reading and reading and reading without sleeping, and the experiment succeeded, though I did not grow any wiser!) In the second year, I left the college for several months and entered an English school so that English would become a living language for me. And thus I became capable of moving in those semi-Egyptian circles and dealing with them with extraordinary efficiency, despite my lack of respect for them. It was a deeply saddening matter to see all these people living in our country, some of whom had never left it, yet they knew nothing about it and did not even speak its language! The English department in Alexandria was a unique experience. Teaching there took the form of real lectures, not dictation lessons. (My memory was strong to the degree that I did not forget anything mentioned in lectures. When I wrote my doctoral dissertation and some of my works on Zionism in English and Arabic, I did not use the customary index cards, despite having read dozens of references and quoted from them. This was because I would remember the quotation and the page on which it appeared. Yet I must note that I am not good at listening to lectures, for I often drift off as a result of an idea the lecturer says and begin to contemplate it.) The professors would enter and deliver their lectures and give the students the opportunity to ask their questions. They would accept the other opinion with an open chest, indeed they would welcome it.

His answer was simple and naive, for he used the concept of simple causality: that every effect has a cause, and this created world must have a Creator, and so the matter is entirely clear. At that point I asked him: and who created evil? His answer was of the utmost simplicity as well, for he said that the mind is incapable of grasping such things. Contemplation began, and I ended up declaring that I would not pray and would not fast until I found an answer to my questions. The members of my family received the news with some disbelief at first, and since they had grown accustomed to such transformations from me (since two years before I had joined the Muslim Brotherhood and used to spend a long time at night reading the Quran with one of the servants), my father cursed me but left me to my own devices. The matter did not stop there, for after the summer passed I moved to Alexandria and met Said al-Basyuni, who had also been shaken by doubt. We began to converse, and I found the location of the Hijaziyya bookshop, whose owner was a cultured man who helped us choose books (unlike the book sellers of these days, who are characterized by utter ignorance, for their interest in the book ends at its price and its color!). The circle of dialogue widened for me, and what made things easier was my presence in Alexandria (and specifically in the Faculty of Arts, English Language and Literature department) with a group of foreigners (Greeks and Italians) who did not hesitate to discuss such matters with extreme freedom, which afforded me the opportunity to pose more questions, until doubt became a fundamental component of my vision.

Since I considered myself a socialist, I filled out a membership card; the application was processed and I became a communist, and I was even prevented from traveling abroad (were it not for my father's intervention). Several years later (after the nationalization of my father's factory), my appointment to a quasi-leadership position was objected to because I was a communist and a capitalist at the same time (and perhaps now the label "Islamist" would be added, making me condemned regardless of the ruling ideology!). When I was in the United States, the formation of what was called the Vanguard Organization began. I was invited to the first meeting, and I raised the issue of the secrecy of this organization, and that was the last meeting I attended. (It is unfortunate that most members of this Vanguard Organization had no socialist or nationalist commitment. Most of them settled in the United States and did not return to the homeland to help build it, as other ordinary students did!) I recall once that I was going to deliver a lecture on the Hegelian dialectic at one of the seminars of the Organization of Arab Students at Syracuse University, and the main theme was socialism. It so happened that one of the students who was the son of one of the members of the ruling socialist elite was there, and when one of his friends told him to attend this seminar, he refused, saying: "We're the socialism people." Among the matters that puzzle me greatly, and puzzle every member of the family, is the reason behind the nationalization of my father's factory. He had been a major merchant who owned his trade and some real estate, and before entering the world of industry he met some senior officials in the revolutionary government, who assured him that what was needed was Egyptian industrialization and that the national bourgeoisie had a role in this. So my father transferred most of his capital from trade and real estate to industry. He sold a huge plot of land he owned in Shatbi (the women's student residence now stands on it) and bought a factory from a foreigner and developed it.

The Tatar king had sent him a message demanding his surrender, using the phrase "O cousin," which apparently carried a meaning of contempt. Qutuz's advisors counseled him to obey the Tatar king's command. But instead, he cut off their heads and hung them on the gates of Cairo. The Egyptians regained confidence in themselves and defeated the Tatar armies at Ain Jalut, stopping this plague that wanted to destroy all human civilizations consciously. In my book on the Palestinian intifada and the Zionist crisis -- A Study in Perception and Dignity -- I show how the intensification of the crisis within the Zionist entity and the growing self-confidence of the Palestinians led to its outbreak, just as Hezbollah's victory in southern Lebanon generated confidence in souls once more, and so the al-Aqsa and Independence Intifada erupted. This does not mean that self-confidence alone is the cause of the intifada, but it is necessary for it. As they say in English: a necessary but not sufficient condition. Rutgers University: New York was full of free cultural opportunities. We lived for some time close to the Cloisters Museum, which is a museum specializing in the arts of the Christian Middle Ages in the West. We also frequented the Metropolitan Museum constantly -- it is not merely a museum but a major cultural-educational institution (like many museums in the West now). In addition, there was a large number of diverse museums (Guggenheim, Frick, the Natural History Museum... etc.). We learned in New York how to eat different kinds of food (Chinese, Japanese, Thai, Indian, Nepali, Italian), in addition to the various botanical and zoological gardens.

And Egypt is now teeming with small battles everywhere -- may God protect us and you from their evil!) But fortunately, my first thesis, which was supposed not to exceed one hundred pages, swelled until it reached five hundred, and it became imperative that I leave the new system and follow the old one. (Yet I must note that the experiment failed; those who underwent it successfully found no work afterwards. The academic bureaucracy in the United States would ask the applicant for a position about his narrow specialization, and when he would mention that he had written three short theses, his application would be rejected.) The same logic explains another incident in my life. I began writing my doctoral dissertation on June 4, 1967, when I realized the magnitude of the catastrophe that had befallen us. At that moment I decided to finish my studies so we could return to contribute what we had to rebuilding the wounded homeland. And the year 1967 for someone residing in the United States did not mean American/Zionist aggression against Egypt alone; it also meant complete American rampaging in Vietnam and the operations of extermination that the American armed forces were carrying out in defense of a corrupt military government and its strategic interests, against an Asian people trying to determine its own destiny. The point is that I decided to submit my doctoral dissertation and then refuse to receive the degree after its discussion and approval, in protest against American behavior in Egypt and Vietnam. But the funny thing is that I thought about my fate in Egypt after returning, for they would say: he failed and he is covering up his failure with this business of protest. I would have tried in vain to defend myself, then I would have tried to obtain the doctorate in Egypt, and I would have entered labyrinths that would have delayed me from my intellectual project, to which I wanted to devote myself. So I abandoned my revolutionary decision (and I did not regret it afterward). And as I said, the department at Rutgers was quite small.

Among my professors I also recall Professor David Weimer, with whom I am still bound by a close friendship. He was the supervisor of my doctoral dissertation. We would meet once or twice a week and discuss everything, and we would walk together in the streets, gardens, and restaurants. I had begun to hold a weekly gathering at one of the cafes in the city of New Brunswick, which I called "Pastoral Friday" (in English: Pastoral Friday) -- meaning a meeting that evokes the ideal atmosphere, free of pain, doubt, and conflict, the world of spontaneity and sound instinct uncorrupted by civilization and unruined by modernity, in which shepherds are assumed to move (in the pastoral songs of the Western tradition). I would meet with my friends and anyone who wished to join us that day, and the basic condition of this gathering was that no one would speak about academic matters and that we would let ourselves go freely, talking, chattering, eating, and smoking cheap cigars. David Weimer would sometimes come to the Pastoral Friday gathering and enjoy it greatly. Professor Weimer helped me and encouraged me through the stages of writing my doctoral dissertation (as I shall explain later). He would get very excited about what I was writing and saw in it much wisdom and some madness, and that the proportion of wisdom was greater than the proportion of madness. He often read what I wrote of research papers to the students. When I presented him with the first copy of my doctoral dissertation, he told me orally that it was a distinguished thesis. When I returned to my office, I found a letter from him written in two lines, saying: let me tell you in this somewhat formal way that you have written a distinguished work.

(The leaflet began with a famous phrase from Melville's short story "Bartleby, the Scrivener": "I would prefer not to.") I stated in the leaflet that the teaching assistants in the English department were being exploited to a degree exceeding the exploitation of teaching assistants in other departments, for we were teaching, grading student papers, and performing other tasks, which made the position of the teaching assistant not merely a research assistant or teaching assistant but a full-time employee. I demanded either the doubling of the salary or the reduction of working hours. A meeting was convened on the basis of my leaflet, attended by all the teaching assistants, and the decision was made to demand cutting the working hours in half. The university president was informed of the decision, and he agreed immediately. Perhaps this was the first (and last) time in history that a revolution was achieved through a single leaflet typed by a secretary working for the ruling institution. In this heated atmosphere, Kavin and I decided to establish a Marxist intellectual forum. I went to the university administration and requested a meeting with the dean of students, as the responsible party, and told him without any evasion what I wanted. And instead of a sharp confrontation between the bourgeoisie (represented in the person of the dean) on the one hand, and the students and revolutionary forces (represented in my humble person) on the other, the dean smiled a broad liberal smile and said: Mr. al-Messiri, we thank you for your suggestion, for we are in dire need of a Marxist party at this university, since no respectable university can exist without such a party. (I was struck with frustration and great vexation. This accursed man spoiled the opportunity for us! Instead of recording a hot historical moment of confrontation between the ascending forces -- us -- and the descending forces -- them -- here we are negotiating with extreme cordiality.) With extreme coolness, he politely asked me about the day on which the "Socialist Forum" would meet and designated the place for me.

The humanity, and the quantity of narcissism in some of them was enormous. I do not mind the existence of a measure of narcissism in human beings, for this is a fundamental matter for them, especially for the revolutionary, as narcissism is a psychological mechanism through which he defends himself against a society that wishes to swallow him. But the narcissism I observed in many of the comrades was indeed extreme, and the moral liberties they allowed themselves were complete -- meaning that in reality they were Nietzschean-Darwinian personalities that had no relation to Marxism or to any moral system, especially since the Marxism of some of them sprang from blind class hatred and not from a belief in the necessity of establishing justice on earth. Indeed, I often felt that some of them were Marxist only by virtue of their class position, and that if the opportunity arose for them to escape their class and join the exploiting oppressive classes, they would have done so without hesitation and divorced their Marxism irrevocably. For all of this, I submitted my resignation and asked to be counted among the friends of the party, not its members. After my departure from the party, one of my female students was arrested on charges of communism; she was married to one of the comrades. Her husband began to flirt with her closest friend (who was also one of my students). I rebuked him and asked him to wait at least until his wife, his comrade in the struggle, was released. He did not heed the advice. But when his wife came out of prison, he divorced her and married her friend in a Darwinian manner that had nothing to do with respect for the human being. When my student came to me complaining about what happened (and she had always mocked me for my ethical and humanistic tendencies, which she called "unscientific"), I said to her sarcastically: you served the previous phase; as for the subsequent phase, it requires a new wife. She burst into tears.

The material and opaque -- meaning he had drowned in informationism. After I left Columbia University to study at Rutgers University, there was a series of simple critical books aimed at helping students study English literature, which paid a fee of five hundred dollars for any critical introduction published in the series (a not inconsiderable sum in the 1960s). I applied to write a study on the English poet William Wordsworth, and John Smith applied to write a book on Coleridge. His application was accepted and mine was rejected. When we inquired about the reason, the publisher was frank and clear: the Arab name would make students refrain from buying the book (and he was right about this). I asked my friend to apply under his name to write the book on Wordsworth, on the understanding that I would be the one to write it. His application was accepted, and I did write it. When his turn came to write the book on Coleridge, he was completely unable to do so, for the informational wolf had attacked him. So I wrote it, but he added some information (which distorted the book, in my estimation). The friendship remained between us for some time, until he submitted his critical works for promotion at his college. The Wordsworth book was accepted and the Coleridge book was rejected. This was bound to make the relationship between us cool considerably, though it continued for some time after that. Immediately after my arrival at Rutgers University, Professor William Phillips joined it, and his fame is owed to his being one of the founders of the Partisan Review, an intellectual journal with a leftist orientation hostile to totalitarianism, which gradually moved away from Marxism while retaining its social, historical, and civilizational sensibility. Professor William Phillips brought his journal with him, and it began to be published from Rutgers University.

Naturally, there is always a gap separating my distinguished students from their parents, and this gap is a source of constant complaint. But the intensity that characterized this girl's discourse is something that still puzzles me. Among the Egyptians I came to know in the United States of America and whose family friendship I cherish are Dr. Ashraf al-Bayoumi and his wife, Dr. Suhair Mursi. Both of them achieved a distinguished scientific standing, and I heard that Dr. Ashraf was considered one of the most important in his field in the United States. Yet despite this, he and his wife returned to Egypt to contribute to building the homeland, and they are among the few Egyptians who did that. For the powerful enticements in the United States and the research possibilities tempt many to stay there. Then they return to us as foreign experts whom we celebrate and crown their heads with laurels, while we forget those who sacrificed and returned because of their national commitment. Dr. Ashraf and his wife are -- in my estimation -- something rare, for together they constitute a revolutionary movement and a driving force for society that inspires optimism, because if two individuals can stir the stagnant waters to this extent and breathe life into society, then it is possible, if efforts combine, to accomplish something. And the revolution in America -- a year after my arrival at Rutgers University, I met Kavin Riley, the contemporary American historian and author of the book The West and the World: A History of Civilization Through Themes. A deep friendship developed between us. Both of us were then Marxists, but we were Marxists "with a caveat," so to speak, for we had many problems with the simple reductive materialist interpretations. We believed in Marxist humanism and cared about the role of thought in history.
