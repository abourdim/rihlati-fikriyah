Beginnings of Identity: Circles of Separation. My mother told me that when I was a child of three or four, they found me walking alone on the balcony overlooking the garden of our house, having put on a pair of eyeglasses and stuck a reed in my mouth in the shape of a cigarette. I held the cigarette in one hand and placed the other behind my back, and began pacing the balcony back and forth with seriousness and gravity. When they asked me what I was doing, I told them that I had seen Dr. Kamil Issa, the family physician, the previous night, and had watched the whole family listening to his advice and instructions. Perhaps this was the first time I performed the rituals of separation from my commercial environment, expressing my desire to become something else. The rituals of separation in their beginnings are always contrived and theatrical (for the person believes in the model before it is realized in reality), especially in traditional societies where the dominant model reigns and does not accept any fundamental challenges. (And so I used to encourage my female students who pretended to be cultured to continue the pretense, and I would claim that I believed them completely, in the hope that the pretense would, after a while, become a second nature and finally a reality.) What helped the separation was that the artistic taste of the members of my family was different from the rest of society, for a reason I do not know to this day. I do not recall ever having listened to Umm Kulthum once in our home, and so you find me to this day unable to master the art of listening to her (and listening to Umm Kulthum, as her admirers tell me, is an art with its own principles). For the same reason, I was among the first to discover Fairuz. I suffered greatly because of this, for her songs were broadcast at strange hours, so I had to either stay up or wake up very early in the morning to hear her. (I do not know whether my love for the voice of Majida al-Roumi and Kazem al-Saher is a continuation of these rituals of separation, or whether it is merely delight in two plaintive voices and in two singers who excel at choosing the texts they sing.)

---

The symbols and rites of separation deepened when one day I discovered the municipal library through the son of one of the employees (for sons of merchants like myself did not go to libraries; they would instead go in the summer to their fathers' shops to work in them, or go to supervise the cotton harvest on the agricultural lands that the major merchants used to buy either for social prestige or for guaranteed investment and securing the future). I remember well that the first thing I encountered were the colored children's books of Mr. Kamil Kilani, the likes of which I had never seen before, and a joy overwhelmed me that I had never felt before. The library custodian, Mr. Zuwail, saw something good in me, and he began to encourage me to read and would choose books for me himself. He advised me to read history books, including Abd al-Rahman al-Rafi'i's book on the modern history of Egypt, and some accessible books on philosophy and the arts, and some novels. I recall that once my eyes fell on the word "Gnosticism" in one of Dr. Abd al-Rahman Badawi's books, and I was struck with a shudder from the sound of the word itself. I read much about it and understood nothing at the time, but I kept trying for the rest of my life. (I was keen, when teaching at the university, to deliver the first lecture in most courses in the library, to tell the students about the borrowing system, the library's divisions, and the types of books: encyclopedias, dictionaries, reference guides, reference books, and art books. Many of the female students would tell me that this lecture constituted a turning point in their lives, exactly like my visit to the Damanhour library.) I began to acquire books, which was a habit unknown among the sons of merchants (my father -- may God have mercy on him -- would always say to me: finish what you have of books and then buy others after that). And so it was not possible for me to ask for the price of the books I bought, which required many maneuvers.

---

I would sometimes even forgo the small sandwich I used to buy during the break from the school canteen in order to buy a book with its price. Through my friendship with the son of the employee, Dr. Muhammad Shuqayr (the doctor who now works in one of Canada's hospitals), an entirely different world opened up before me. His father worked as the headmaster of the agricultural school. I noticed that he and his family were less wealthy economically than my family, yet their way of life was more beautiful. I would see him reading books, and when I went to their home, I noticed that they talked about many varied things, and there were paintings on the wall and curios in the silver cabinet (I remember in particular a small bottle of deep, deep blue into which I would plunge when I looked into it, and I still feel a profound weakness toward the color blue). I began to realize that what determines a person's life is not necessarily the economic factor. All of these experiences that I underwent as a child or young boy could have turned into mere personal experiences without my grasping their social significance, and without my generalizing analytical models from them, and without their helping me enter the world of thought, had God not blessed me with teachers (and university professors) who helped me, pushed me, supported my self-confidence, and helped me with critical thinking (and self-confidence is necessary so that a person can generalize and formulate interpretive models). At the beginning of the secondary stage, religious tendencies began to grow within me, so I joined the Muslim Brotherhood (as I mentioned before), and the first person I guided to faith was a Nubian cook who worked for us. We would spend most of the night reading the Quran, and I would give the call to prayer at the Muslim Brotherhood branch in Damanhour. Then my activity extended to the village of Iflaqa near Damanhour. Although I was no more than twelve years old, the villagers agreed to let me lead them in prayer and deliver the Friday sermon.

---

This process continued for several months, until one of the Brothers alerted me that this was a matter that contravened the Sharia due to my young age (although I had studied religious jurisprudence through a number of books, especially the book Fiqh al-Sunnah by Sheikh Sayyid Sabiq). This period of my life was short (no more than two years), for questions rained down upon me that began to undermine my simple and direct faith -- as I shall explain later. I spent the secondary school stage at the Damanhour Secondary School. There was a large number of young teachers who wished to continue their postgraduate studies in Alexandria and had not been appointed at the university, and so Damanhour was an extremely suitable place for them; it is only 60 kilometers from Alexandria, and they could reside or work there and travel to Alexandria to prepare their university dissertations. Among my most important teachers were Mr. Shafiq, the geography teacher, Mr. Ghazlan, the physics teacher, and Mr. Rofael, the history teacher, who saw something good in me (without any precedent on my part or any evidence from my academic record) and announced to the students that I was a genius and that they should not compare themselves to me. He began to ask me to write research papers beyond the curriculum. When I would finish them, he would read them to the students, which caused me great embarrassment and immense happiness at the same time. I did not understand the secret of his enthusiasm for me. For until that point (third year of secondary school), my feeling was that my intelligence was ordinary and perhaps below ordinary. My academic performance testified to this: failure in the third year of primary school and passing from the second round, an extremely low aggregate score in the primary certificate and repeating the first year of secondary school, failure in the second year of secondary school and passing once again from the second round, extremely low grades, a deep hatred of mathematics and the English language, and private tutoring at a time when such a phenomenon was not yet known.

---

Yet despite all this, Mr. Rofael decided that I had something, and so I found myself obliged not to disappoint him and to strike the flint of my mind so as to come up with brilliant things, as was expected of me. My academic performance improved after that with a speed that astonished me personally. As for Mr. Emil George (Dr., now), he was the real beginning of my intellectual life. He was a teacher in the full sense of the word. We studied philosophy under him in the final year (1954/1955), and he made us love his subject. He would present the deepest philosophical questions to us in a simple manner, and he would sow doubt in our minds, but he would not cast us into the abyss of nihilism -- he was an excellent teacher. When I meet him these days and converse with him, I find in him a renewed vitality and a forward-thinking mind, and I realize the importance of the teacher, for without him I would have wasted years upon years, reading what I read without reaching the depths, accumulating information without grasping its dimensions and meaning. My experience with education in Egypt was extremely happy (with the exception of those accursed arithmetic lessons). How great was my happiness when the time came to receive books at the beginning of the year, and I still remember what I read in the history, geography, and philosophy books! In addition to study and learning at the hands of teachers who loved their subjects and conveyed them in a manner endearing to students, there was leisure time in which we would frolic and play, alongside classes in sports, crafts, drawing, music, agriculture, and calligraphy. I shudder now when I think about what happens to our young children in schools and our youth in universities, who are shackled with heavy informational books (printed in a shabby manner), who spend all their time studying subjects they forget after a month passes, and who are left no room for play or breathing, and who encounter in the classroom teachers who turn the school lesson into a pretext for herding students toward private tutoring. (When my son returned from the United States with his sister in 1984, he knew only English.

---

So we did not mind, naturally. But we were surprised by a telephone call from his sister informing us that Yasser had failed the English language exam. I was somewhat confused and asked her: is the English language the English language?! When the answer came in the affirmative, I knew that the Egyptian reception ceremony had begun. I later learned that the examining teacher was eager to give my son remedial tutoring so he could pass the exam, and we submitted to the reality of the situation. God alone is the Strong. Education in Egypt used to be free and enjoyable, and gradually it became non-free because of private tutoring, and then it ceased to have any relation to education, as education has now become the acquisition of the ability to pass examinations.) The school was -- as I said -- a truly rich and enjoyable experience, yet I must mention what happened in the philosophy subject in the final year. Out of my intense love for it and my excellence in it, I would explain to my friends what was obscure in its meanings. They all received high marks in the final exam, especially Farouk al-Messiri (may God have mercy on him), the son of my father's cousin, who received the highest philosophy score in the entire country -- 77/50 in 1960. As for me, I received 41, which is the minimum required to pass. It seems that final-year students are not expected to give their own opinion on Francis Bacon, for example, as I did. (Perhaps this is the secret behind my failure in the drawing subject, as I decided to be creative and original -- and there is no power nor strength except in God.) Something similar happened to my daughter in the GCSE certificate year.

---

She received the distinction. I met the British cultural attache and pointed out to him the gravity of this situation: that the school was turning into an institution for the flattening of minds and personalities. It seems that this is the general trend in the world, and it is part of the process of rationalization and standardization that has accelerated in recent times. I learned from these experiences that success and failure in public life according to prevailing standards are not necessarily an accurate or final judgment, and that a person may fail by prevailing standards but may succeed by standards that are more authentic and creative. Symbols and Rituals and the Malady of Contemplation: There are many elements in my personality that helped deepen my separation from my surroundings and generated in me a constant desire to philosophize and interpret anything that happens to me, and not to accept it at face value -- which ultimately led to the emergence of the concept of distance (which I shall explain later). The first of these elements is that certain things would acquire a symbolic value in my mind beyond their functional value. Macaroni, for instance, was for me magic itself (I used to imagine in my childhood that it was the food of the people of Paradise), and so eating it meant a quasi-spiritual experience that had nothing to do with satisfying the biological need for food. I would eat it not according to my material nutritional need but according to my psychological, emotional, or even spiritual need, if you will (and so I used to look with a degree of understanding at the case of Khedive Abbas II, who is said to have been controlled by his foreign advisors through macaroni. I also understood the case of King Farouk, who is said to have suffered a heart attack after consuming a huge quantity of macaroni.) As for rice, it was associated in my mind with tranquility and with the return to the city. And so after returning from a school trip I would ask my mother to cook me some rice, and she would offer me all kinds of food. But to no avail.

---

For rice after the trip was no longer a food with which I filled my stomach, but rather a matter of symbolic significance; and she could not understand my symbolic world, just as I could not accept her functional logic. I never freed myself from this inclination toward symbolization. The cigar became a symbol of calm, stability, and accomplishment. Often the theses of the books I write acquire a symbolic dimension, making them part of the human being's battle with everything that threatens him. For example, the Encyclopedia transformed into the battle of the human being against injustice and into this eternal struggle between the human human (who tries to transcend the world of the five senses) and the natural/material human, who squats in it, contented and satisfied. I believe that this inclination toward symbolization helped me greatly to separate from my immediate environment, for the symbols created a world of my own. Moreover, the symbol is without doubt a form of the model, for it is an element from the material world, but it rises above it until it becomes a concentrated sign for many elements that may at first glance seem to have no relation to one another. Connected to this tendency toward symbolization is what I call the ritualistic tendency, for I tend to make every important event in my life part of a very particular ritual that I develop myself. In my childhood, I would begin my studying by placing a flower in a vase, or dreaming of it if there was no flower. When I grew older, I developed the concept of "non-biological tea": any cup of tea that I do not need from a material standpoint, yet which I drink with my friend to enjoy his company. (This later evolved into the concept of "non-biological fatherhood," when I adopt some orphans from the victims of the modern age.) When my father passed on to God's mercy, I mentioned the special rituals I performed in New York (watching Brecht's play The Exception and the Rule).
