Cognitive Models and Analytical Models: From Receptive Objectivity to Ijtihadi Objectivity. The process of transition from materialism to humanism and faith was not an easy or simple matter, and many of my friends did not believe what happened at first; some of them cut me off, and my relationship with some others shrank. Because my writings are rationalist (even though their ultimate frame of reference is one of faith: faith in God and in the human being as a non-material creature who acquires his complexity from being a divine creature, not a natural one), some continued to consider me a materialist, because they linked rationality with materialism -- a linkage that has no basis in reality. Robespierre was a pure materialist who proclaimed the worship of reason, yet at the same time he imposed a reign of terror on the French people for a period that did not end until he himself was sent to the guillotine (exactly like Danton before him, who was struck with disgust at this material terrorist rationality and said while before the guillotine: "I prefer that the guillotine cut off my head rather than that I cut off the heads of others. I feel nauseous from the human race."). Hitler was a materialist, extreme in his materialism, and at the same time an irrationalist, extreme in his irrationalism -- and so was Stalin. Can it be claimed that Western imperialism -- this movement hostile to the human being and to reason, which burned everything in its path and exterminated millions on the basis of the claim of white supremacy -- can it be claimed that this material movement is rational? The change in religious and intellectual vision was accompanied by a change in the philosophy of methodology and its tools. It is impossible for one to occur without the other. When I shook materialism off my thought, it became difficult for me to accept the conception that the human mind is a blank slate that records reality passively and directly, as if the human being were merely a material thing among things.

When I shook materialism off my thought, it became difficult for me to accept the conception that the human mind is a blank slate that records reality passively and directly, as if the human being were merely a material thing among things. Three fundamental, interconnected, simultaneous themes appeared in my life, to the point that I almost say they are three faces of a single coin (if the expression is permissible), expressing my transformation from the materialist model to the model that separates the human being from nature/matter. These themes are: the transition from photographic objectivity (receptive, documentary, materialist, and value-free) to ijtihadi objectivity; the rejection of the passive mind and the adoption of a generative view of the mind; and finally, the rejection of direct observation and the adoption of the model as a method of analysis. Despite the interconnection of these three elements, I shall -- as a methodological tactic -- address them one after the other. Let me begin with the first theme, namely the transition from photographic (receptive) and informational objectivity to ijtihadi objectivity. Photographic objectivity is an analytical model that holds that knowledge is a cumulative process consisting of capturing the greatest possible number of details of (material) reality as it is, approximately, in a photographic (or quasi-photographic) manner, and incorporating them into the research or study (without linking the information together and without attempting to abstract patterns from them). The objective has been defined as that which bears the same relation to all different individual observers.

The objective has been defined as that which bears the same relation to all different individual observers. Objectivity is based on the premise that there are existing relations among the parts of perceived things, and that all people are capable of perceiving these relations in the same way if they are placed in the correct position for perceiving them. I do not care which definition one adopts; what matters is the cognitive model latent behind it. In the case of objectivity, we find that the cognitive model equalizes all minds, and so if the conditions are right, perception is uniform -- that is, objective. Such a definition abolishes the effectiveness of the mind and its creativity and its limits, and abolishes historical memory and the burdens of moral perceptions and its biases and illusions and hopes and pains and dreams, all of which influence the process of perception. The mind, according to this model, is a passive, simple thing like a camera, trying to encompass all of reality and convey all the details of reality in their entirety, as if it were incapable of deletion, selection, magnification, marginalization, distortion, and falsification -- its ultimate frame of reference being material reality as it is. This conception of the mind and reality neglects the relationship of the part to the whole, of reality to the pattern, of the apparent to the hidden. For the whole, the pattern, and the hidden do not exist in reality; rather, they are frameworks that the active mind abstracts. (As one of the senior professors specializing in methodology told me at a dinner party, after placing his palm on his head: knowledge is the attempt to convey reality photographically, and the more precise the image, the more objective it is; it reflects reality accurately. While he was speaking, I found his head suddenly transforming before me into a square with a lens in the middle rotating in all directions. I laughed. When he asked me why I was laughing, I told him: I just remembered that I don't own a camera, which affects my objectivity. He looked at me in astonishment, and his photographic apparatus did not register the meaning of my words!)

Informationism -- the conception that knowledge is the stuffing and accumulation of information -- is completely linked to this objective/photographic vision. Informationism holds that information is important in itself, not because of its relation to the overall subject or to a recurring pattern. And so authorship becomes the author's marshaling of the greatest amount of information regardless of its lack of coherence and the absence of a central focus. The latent assumption is that the more information there is, the greater the degree of proximity to reality (as it is) -- until the researcher marshals all the information or references (or most of them) and gives us a carbon copy of reality. This is a conception that implies an image of the mind as a passive entity. This objective, receptive, informational stance is not objective but rather "objectal" -- meaning that the researcher contents himself with monitoring details and subjects and recording them without linking them together, without showing which are central and which are marginal, which are expressive of the overall pattern and which are merely unrepresentative facts, which deserve to be retained and which deserve to be excluded. And so I also speak of the difference between thought and ideas. Thought is the act of linking different ideas together and then reassembling them within a specific system that is characterized by a degree of abstraction and internal consistency. Ideas, on the other hand, are the recording of one idea after another without attempting to see the overarching unity latent behind the multiplicity.

I also speak of the difference between realism and factualism. Realism is reaching the essence of reality (past, present, and future), and from this starting point one can link different facts, arrange them, and abstract from them a general meaning that transcends each piece of information on its own. Factualism, however, is tied to the present alone; it is a direct observation of the existing state of affairs that neglects what is latent. And so we find that factualism in our Arab world, which presents itself as realism, leads to the negation of history and to distress, gloom, and defeat. The advocates of normalization and globalization always claim that they are realists, while in truth they are factualists. The real realists are the mujahideen in southern Lebanon and the resisters in Palestine and the revolutionaries in Iraq, who transcended the apparent and reached the hidden (the latent possibility) and moved within its framework, and the event occurred: they inflicted defeat on the enemy and victory became a reality! Perhaps the distinction between objectivity and objectality, realism and factualism, thought and ideas, goes back to this distinction that I always advocate: between facts and truth. Facts are scattered material data not connected by any link. Truth, on the other hand, is the product of a human intellectual effort when the mind links facts together and then abstracts a model from them. The two processes of linking and abstraction stand at the antipode of the two processes of marshaling and accumulation.

The two processes of linking and abstraction stand at the antipode of the two processes of marshaling and accumulation. (And naturally, if there is a difference between truth and facts, there is a difference between both of them on the one hand and the Right/the True on the other; for the Right precedes the processes of understanding, perception, analysis, abstraction, deconstruction, and reconstruction.) Among the funniest jokes about receptive objectivity, which entirely abolishes the mind, is the joke that Dr. Osama el-Baz told me when we were studying together in the United States: a beggar walked through the city announcing that he was going to marry the sultan's daughter. No one paid him any attention, but when he persisted in his claim for several days, someone grabbed him by the scruff of his neck and said: "Why are you spreading these lies, you beggar?" He said: "In reality, the matter is nearly settled. I have agreed to this marriage, and so have my father and mother. All that remains is the consent of the sultan's daughter and her father and her mother." I used to ask my female students why we laugh at this story even though the beggar is truthful in what he says. Through dialogue, we would arrive at the conclusion that the beggar, indeed, from a receptive objective standpoint, did not lie -- he and his parents represent 50% of the objective elements constituting the phenomenon. But the matter changes entirely if we take into account the degree of value and effectiveness of each element (which requires the exercise of mind and imagination), for we will then conclude that the decision of the beggar and his parents to marry the sultan's daughter has no value. At the monthly seminar I hold at my home, my student and friend Yasser Alawi gave another example. He said: two detectives entered a room where a crime had occurred and cast a glance at it. After a while, one of them noted the following information: the victim's corpse, a recently used pistol, an empty wallet, a green button. The first detective compiled this information and concluded that there was a murder committed with a pistol for the purpose of robbery, and that the killer was wearing a green shirt.

He said: two detectives entered a room where a crime had occurred and cast a glance at it. After a while, one of them noted the following information: the victim's corpse, a recently used pistol, an empty wallet, a green button. The first detective compiled this information and concluded that there was a murder committed with a pistol for the purpose of robbery, and that the killer was wearing a green shirt. As for the second detective, he continued the process of objective observation and began to note: two chairs, the diameter of the table, a painting, the color of the curtain, the color of the ceramic, the height of the wall... etc. The facts recorded by the second detective are solid facts -- there is no disputing this (they are no less solid than the significant information noted by the first detective) -- but he did not use his mind in the process of linking and abstraction that leads to choosing some elements and excluding others. And so he was lost in the midst of abundant, precise, unconnected information that has no explanatory value! The parable shows that the increase of information does not necessarily lead to an increase in knowledge and wisdom! I also used to tell my female students a comic story from the Juha tales that sheds light on receptive objectivity. Juha went to one of the villages and claimed to be a religious scholar, so the villagers honored him. He sat in the mosque worshiping and devouring whatever food was brought to him. After several days, the villagers wanted to benefit from his abundant knowledge. After their insistence, Juha stood in the middle of the mosque to preach to them and asked: "Have you heard the discourse about Paradise and its people?" They said: "No." Signs of anger appeared on his face and he said: "How do you expect someone of my learning to speak to those of your ignorance?" He sat down to resume his worship and devouring of food. The villagers were saddened and decided to change their answer. They went to Juha again, asking him for knowledge and sermons.

The villagers were saddened and decided to change their answer. They went to Juha again, asking him for knowledge and sermons. After their insistence, he stood up again and asked: "Have you heard the discourse about Paradise and its people?" They said: "Yes." Signs of pleasure and delight appeared on his face and he said: "Praise be to God! Praise be to God! You are people of knowledge and piety. So enjoy your knowledge and piety and your acquaintance with the discourse about Paradise and its people!" He sat down to resume his worship and devouring of food. The villagers were at a loss about him and decided to follow a new plan. They went to him and insisted that he preach to them. Juha stood up and said: "Have you heard the discourse about Paradise and its people?" Half the villagers said: "Yes." The other half said: "No." And Juha simply said: "Those who know can tell those who do not know." He sat down and returned to what he had been doing. The female students would laugh at the story. But they usually failed to explain the reason for the laughter. After a while, however, we would agree that Juha equated knowledge (the complex product of linking and abstraction) with information (the simple, direct, isolated from any context). The discourse about Paradise was for him merely a piece of information: either you know it or you don't. His questions resembled the questions in an objective exam, the answer to which is either yes or no -- which is as far from the truth as can be. The poor villagers swallowed the bait of receptive objectivity and sat in the mosque after their defeat, despised and dejected.
