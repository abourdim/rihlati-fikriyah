Within the framework of this conception, it became inevitable that I would look at Egypt not as a static example of this or that quality, but rather as a moment in a paradigmatic sequence whose links follow one another, such that I use what I see in the West on the assumption that it is likely to recur here with us, for the same premises and social circumstances may lead to the same results or something close to them, and they undoubtedly serve as an indicator of what might happen in the future. What comes to mind here is what Serge Latouche said in his book The Westernization of the World: for him, the West is not a geographical spot nor even a temporal moment, but rather a paradigmatic sequence that has developed and taken different forms until it became like a machine that cares little for the human being, spinning to grind everyone, even its own master, detached from Western time and space, capable of seizing any place and time. Who could have imagined in the past that what is happening now in Egypt could ever happen? Who could have imagined that money would become the standard that overrides all other standards and that the matter of knowledge would become a source of mockery? When my wife and I returned from the United States in 1974, some taxi drivers would refuse to accept fare from us when they learned that we were university professors who had returned to our country to contribute to its building and development — can we imagine such a thing happening at the present time? In short, I do not see that East is East and West is West, or that the East is spiritual and the West is material, and so on with these ready-made sayings. Rather, I see that there is a paradigmatic sequence that, if it seizes a civilization, begins to materialize (unless the human being confronts it with human and moral consciousness). The idea of the paradigmatic sequence appears as a fundamental analytical tool in most of my writings. But it appears, in particular, in my analysis of hulul (monistic immanence) and comprehensive secularism.

The paradigmatic sequence — and through the prism of this sequence I was able to look at the West, and at Egypt too, in a new light. In the West I would see things and phenomena, and I would say to myself: this will happen in Egypt in such-and-such a number of years. And indeed, events would prove me right. And the notion of the paradigmatic sequence is linked to another notion which is that of cognitive models. For the human being does not see reality directly; rather, he sees it through cognitive models that intervene between him and reality, shaping his perception and directing his interpretation. These cognitive models are not neutral lenses; they are loaded with assumptions and biases and cultural sedimentations. And the human being is usually unaware of these models — he thinks he is seeing reality as it is, when in fact he is seeing it through these models. And this is what I call fiqh al-tahayyuz (the jurisprudence of bias), which means that every knowledge and every methodology contains within it a bias that must be uncovered and dealt with consciously. And the concept of bias is not necessarily negative — it is the human element in knowledge. The problem arises when the human being is unaware of his bias and imagines himself to be completely objective and neutral.

---

And within this framework I developed what I call compound models — that is, analytical models that are capable of accommodating the complexity of the human phenomenon without reducing it to a single dimension. The simple model reduces the human being to the natural/material, and sees him as a creature driven by one motive — whether economic or sexual or the will to power. But the compound model recognizes that the human being is a complex creature who cannot be reduced to what is below him. And this is what I tried to do in the Encyclopedia — I tried to develop compound models capable of accommodating the complexity of Jewish and Zionist phenomena without reducing them to a single explanation. And the concept of the functional community is one of these compound models, for it does not explain the situation of Jews in terms of a Jewish essence or a conspiracy, but rather in terms of their social function within the societies in which they lived. And this model can be applied to other groups — the Chinese in Southeast Asia, the Indians in East Africa, the Armenians in the Ottoman Empire, and so on. What distinguishes this model is that it places the Jewish phenomenon within a general human framework and does not treat it as a unique phenomenon outside the scope of human analysis.

---

And from the most important things I learned from my experience at Columbia University, and from my doctoral thesis, is the importance of the analytical framework. For the analytical framework is what determines which information is important and which is marginal, and what the relationships between the data are. And the same information can have completely different meanings depending on the framework used to interpret it. Take for example the numbers of Jewish immigration to Palestine. These numbers in themselves do not mean much. But if we place them in the context of immigration patterns in general, and in the context of European colonial settlement, and in the context of the Zionist project — then these numbers acquire a completely different meaning. And this is what I mean by saying that information without a framework is blind, and a framework without information is empty. And the problem with most studies of Zionism in the Arab world was that they were either all information without a framework, or all framework without information.

---

And I remember that when I began studying the Zionist phenomenon seriously, I was struck by the extent to which the Western narrative dominated the discourse — even the Arab discourse about the conflict. The Palestinians were presented as objects of charity, not as a people with rights. And the Zionist narrative presented itself as a national liberation movement, while it was in reality a settler-colonial movement. And to understand this, one needed analytical tools that went beyond the direct political discourse. One needed to understand the deep structure of the Western colonial project and its relationship to the Zionist project. And this is what I tried to do in my writings — to place the Zionist phenomenon within the broader context of Western modernity and its crisis, and to show that Zionism is not an anomaly in Western history but rather one of its logical products.

---

And the notion of comprehensive secularism is central to my intellectual project. Comprehensive secularism is not merely the separation of religion from the state — that is partial secularism, which I do not necessarily oppose. Comprehensive secularism is a complete worldview in which nature/matter becomes the ultimate reference point for everything. In this worldview, the human being is reduced to the natural/material, and all values and meanings are derived from within this closed system. There is no transcendence, no beyond, no sacred that is separate from the profane. And this comprehensive secularism is what I see as the real danger — not partial secularism, which can coexist with faith and with moral values. And I believe that comprehensive secularism is the logical endpoint of the paradigmatic sequence that I described — it is the moment when the machine has ground everything, including the human being himself.

---

And I found that Western literary criticism, despite its sophistication, suffered from the same disease of passive objectivity that I had observed in other fields. For literary criticism in the West had become increasingly technical and formalistic, concerned with the surface of the text and not with its human meaning. And the various schools of criticism — structuralism, post-structuralism, deconstruction — all shared this characteristic of distancing themselves from the human content of the text. And I saw in this a manifestation of comprehensive secularism in the literary field — the death of meaning, the death of the author, the death of the subject. And I felt that my task as a literary critic was to resist this tendency and to insist on the human dimension of literary texts.

And I began to realize that the concept of bias is present everywhere. When I was working at King Saud University (Department of English Language and Literature), one of the professors submitted his research for promotion. And I realized that we did not grasp that the Hilaliyya epic, for example, is not a lyrical or even a narrative work, but rather a theatrical work of the first order in which dramatic performance mixes with narrative storytelling and lyrical passages. And so I wondered: perhaps if we studied Japanese theater (Noh and Kabuki plays) we would discover a completely different theatrical world, and our vision of theater would change, for it is a theater in which the audience does not sit facing the performers but rather mixes with them completely, just as literary genres blend within it in a wonderful way. And perhaps if we studied Japanese theater (and Indian and Chinese and other non-Western theatrical forms) the history of modern Arab theater would have taken a completely different turn, and perhaps we would have discovered the theatrical forms around us (the peep-show box, shadow puppetry, the Hilaliyya epic, other heroic epics). I mention this to recount the following incident. I was in the courtyard square in Marrakech, moving among the performers, vendors, and storytellers. A storyteller caught my attention — he was telling the story of our master Ali, may God honor his countenance. He was holding a rope in one hand and a stone in the other. When the snake attacked our master Ali, the rope would transform into a speckled serpent, and at other times it would transform into a straight road, and so on. But I noticed that the stone would sometimes fall from his hand, and we would look at it and neglect everything else. Gradually I realized that he was dropping the stone deliberately to change the scene, and that what we were watching was not a narrative or lyrical work but rather a theatrical work that we could not classify as such because of our pre-existing Western biases. And I began to realize that bias exists everywhere.

The human being — as I see it — is a complex phenomenon that cannot be reduced to what is below it: nature/matter, for example, which is governed by simple material, mathematical/mechanical models whose laws are fixed, predictable, and controllable. Therefore, it is necessary to use analytical and interpretive models instead of direct recording as a method of observation, because they are a complex method capable of accommodating and analyzing the human phenomenon. Then I proceed to address the question of hulul (monistic immanence), where I argue that hulul is a gradual process that ends with pantheism, where the duality that distinguishes human existence is eliminated and the distance between Creator and created is abolished. And material pantheism — meaning the sovereignty of the natural/material law over both the human being and nature — is itself, in my view, comprehensive secularism. And this volume will also address various topics related to the subject of hulul, such as: Sabianism, Qadianism, the Greater Man, Spinoza and Hegel and their relation to Western hulul, Nietzsche, hulul and Western philosophy, Gnosticism, the Kabbala, Jewish hulul, Ibn Rushd, Ibn Arabi, the relationship of hulul to functional communities and comprehensive secularism, hulul and the end of history, hulul and postmodernity. And with the escalation of the rates of immanence, the center of the world becomes the human being and nature/matter, and so partial and comprehensive secularism appear, which will be addressed in the second and third volumes. (Which I hastened to publish before the first and fourth volumes in the hope that I would re-pose the question of secularism and open the door of ijtihad regarding it). And with the duality of the human being and nature/matter, both humanism and naturalism/materialism appear, which usually tilts in favor of matter, and the centers of material immanence multiply and shift from economics to sex or the will or the leader... and so on.

Individual religious faith, because this is the age of religious heroism within the Protestant framework. And after this, with the emergence of material rationalism and the scientific vision, it became impossible for anyone to write an epic. Therefore, we find that most poets in the Neoclassical age in Europe (the eighteenth century) dreamed of writing an epic because critical theory placed the epic at the top of the pyramid of literary works, but what was written of epics was rigid and extremely tedious. And when Alexander Pope attempted to write an epic, he wrote a counter-epic, a satirical anti-heroic epic, which is the poem The Rape of the Lock, in which the poet uses all the conventions of the heroic epic to describe a non-heroic world, the world of the eighteenth century where everyone wears their perfumed, excessively elegant and affected clothing and lives their lives as if they were ballet dancers! And the result is a mockery of a beautiful, narrow society that at the same time reminds us of the real, expansive world of heroism that has passed. For in the age of reason and enlightenment (and the secularization of the human being) there is no room for transcendence or heroism. Then the Romantic revolution appeared. And when poets attempted to write an epic, it always took the form of autobiography, for heroism is the struggle of the Romantic poet to realize his self and the world around him and the relationship between them. And so, transcendence is achieved through self-enclosure. And here we are not talking, in reality, about an epic but rather about lyric poetry aspiring to be an epic. Then Byron wrote the poem Don Juan, in which he speaks of the epic hero and his impossibility in the age of utilitarianism and material rationalism — and thus the epic died.

Identity, for it is the moment of the end of history and the realization of the earthly paradise. And I noticed Whitman's oscillation between self and object. He is a subjective poet, deeply immersed in subjectivity, but he took pleasure in losing his self completely in what he sees and contemplates. Therefore, he uses what he himself called the catalogue: to mention the things around him without arrangement or re-shaping through the imagination; for the object that transcends the human being (not the human being who transcends the object) has the final word. And gradually I discovered the relationship between the end of history (and this fall into objectivity) and the absence of moral sense, and that the abolition of history in America (the settler state) means in reality the legitimization of the extermination of the original (historical) population element so that the settlers can begin their history from point zero. For hostility toward history is in reality hostility toward the human being. And I concluded from my comparison between the two poets that Walt Whitman, whom they call in the United States the poet of American democracy, is in reality the poet of totalitarianism and fascism and the death of history and the human being. In the third part of the thesis (the compound thesis) I proposed that we reconsider the matter of influence in light of the difference in visions, and I showed that the influence is real, material, and tangible, but superficial, because the structure of Wordsworth's thought and his vision (his epistemological model) did not affect Whitman at all, and that the (intellectual and cultural) difference between them is more important than the (direct, material) similarity. As for the fourth and final section, which I called the Practice, I wrote it in a somewhat humorous and satirical manner, as is evident from its title: Twenty Ways in Which the Entire Human Race Can Benefit from My Doctoral Thesis, and I concluded it with the same phrase with which the Communist Manifesto concludes, but after modifying it: Workers of the world — for all this, unite (and I had intended to delete it in the final version).
