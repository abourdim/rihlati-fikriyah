Damanhour is what made my mother unable to grasp the new sensibility that had begun to appear: the desire for pleasure in itself, without a moral or practical purpose. And so she loved the large peach tree because it gave us its fruit. As for roses, they posed a problem for her. We would try to decorate the house with them, and she did not object. But she would demand that we make rose jam from some of them! She considered our going to the cinema a waste of time. So we would concoct the usual excuses so we could escape the grip of this vision. For example, I recall that I was enamored with the Zorro serial. (Adventure films used to be shown as serials, and the episode would stop at a critical moment where the hero -- or the "brave one of the cinema" as we used to call him -- or the heroine, or both, were threatened with danger. And naturally the hero, given his well-known extraordinary physical and mental abilities, could escape.) To justify our going to watch it, we would assure our mother that it "encourages noble morals" -- we said it in classical Arabic so she would be convinced and give us the piasters needed to dash off to the municipal cinema. (Foreign films were shown on the screen and there was another small screen beside it on which the translation appeared.) Perhaps the fact that Damanhour is a city/village, modern/old, is revealed through a phenomenon such as medical practice. For scientific medicine (which we practice now) was well-known, and doctors who were graduates of the Faculty of Medicine practiced their profession. And the medical assistants who gave painful injections (usually containing fortifying oils) practiced their trade with all the force and sadism they could muster. When I was a child, I went to Alexandria to have the adenoids in my nose removed, which had been causing me difficulty in breathing. But alongside all this, there was herbal medicine, and the bonesetter was an essential figure, and there was the hakim who knew the families and their medical histories and knew most of the individuals in his community.

The game depended on the players and their skill alone. And so it narrowed the social gap between the players. Moreover, they were all collective games that no individual could play alone (unlike modern expensive games that one can play by oneself, until we reach the pinnacle, which is the computer with which one can play chess alone!). When we grew a little older and left the stage of childhood, we would play games like seega, chess, backgammon, and card games. And of course football (the "sock ball" as it used to be called, which gradually transformed into "the football" or the inflated ball, which is the ball used now in playing football). I also witnessed in early childhood the peepshow box, for a man would come carrying a box with four openings fitted with lenses, behind which was a strip of paper bearing images of Abu Zayd al-Hilali and Antar and Abla. We would sit on a wooden bench that the man carried, and we would place our faces against the lenses, and then the man would begin turning the strip and telling some stories. There was also what was called the "afiya" (the rhyme game). It would begin with an informational sentence or a word or a question posed by competitor (A), to which competitor (B) would respond with the word "ishmiana" (why so?), and then (A) would respond with a comment from a pre-chosen domain, provided the comment was comically biting. Then the roles would reverse, so (B) would say an informational sentence and (A) would say "ishmiana." The competition would continue until one of the competitors ran out of fuel. For example, the competition could take place within the "afiya of films" as follows: (A) You walk down the street with your family, and people say: (B) Why so? (A) Birds of Darkness. Then the roles reverse as follows: (B) Your mother walks down the street, people call her: (A) Why so? (B) Godzilla. Then the roles reverse once more: (A) Your father walks down the street, people call him: (B) Why so?

As for my mother, she was utterly indifferent to this matter of capitalist accumulation and would always express her contempt for wealth that keeps piling up while at the same time driving her husband away from his family (as he was constantly traveling). (How many times I saw him sitting by the door, weeping because he could not stop himself from running and accumulating -- and my mother would stand there, comforting him, until he dried his tears, then he would leap from his place to resume the race.) Perhaps my mother's influence explains my refusal to work in commerce, despite my father's various attempts to get me to work with him in it. I recall that when I decided to marry Dr. Huda Hegazy, I went to him to finance this marriage, and he wanted to use this situation to pressure me. He told me I could marry Juliet (as he put it) if I agreed to work with him. I said: "But I want to study poetry." He said he had no objection to my going abroad to get a master's in poetry, and then returning to work with him in commerce. I agreed, but I came back to him after an hour and told him I had changed my mind, and that it was up to him to approve the financing or refuse it. He was generous, so he acquiesced and agreed. This traditional compassionate spirit has remained firmly rooted in my conscience. After my arrival in the United States in 1963, I was offered an appearance in a television advertisement for shoes. What was required was that I put on a new shoe (which would become mine afterward), then walk in a room, and everyone would look at my shoe with great admiration. Sex had not yet become a fundamental element in advertisements, and so there was no beauty falling in love with me as the shoe-wearer. In any case, I refused to participate in this farce because I would have become a thing that sells itself according to a specific contract. Perhaps the same compassionate spirit appears in the way I accept gifts. For when someone would give me a wrapped gift, I would take it as it was, thank the giver, and not unwrap it.

But when we reached Naples, I discovered that storage was extremely expensive and that it would cost me more than the cost of the trip I had intended to take across Europe. I was at a complete loss and did not know what to do. At that point, one of the porters saw me, and with the help of an English-Italian dictionary and by way of my knowledge of Latin (I would take Latin words and remove their endings, and they would become Italian most of the time), I explained my situation to him. He in turn explained it to the storage clerk, and they decided to alter the weight, and instead of the storage cost being a hundred dollars per day, it became only ten dollars, which was a reasonable price (and yet, multiplied by 171 days, the amount rises to become a respectable sum in the 1960s, indeed a small fortune for a scholarship student and his wife). The human being in his private life and his public life is growing ever more severe despite the rising rates of modernization and rationalization, because of the corruption of many of the ruling elites in the Arab world. For they give the signal to people that the domain of public life is not subject to any moral values, and that belief in civic ethics is a kind of quixotism that can destroy a person!) In a study entitled "The Strange-Spirited Boys: A Study of the Response of the Arab Literary Conscience to the Process of Modernization as Revealed in Three Short Stories," I addressed the question of how the past and traditions become a burden on our modern reality through an analysis of a story by Tawma al-Khuri, the Lebanese writer: "We Are Your Men." The story begins in a very modern atmosphere -- election season, as citizens participate in the process of decision-making. But after the first sentence, the writer uses two images: he compares the unusual activity of the villages during elections to well-beaten eggs, and he likens the alleys of those villages to beehives. That is, the emotional movement here is from the modern era, built on individualism, to the traditional society, built on loyalty to the group. After these two images, the writer returns once again to speak of the importance of the elections and the importance of every vote cast in them, and for this reason voters arrive using every possible means of transportation: donkeys, bulls, camels, lorries, buses, and any vehicle of any kind. Things thus overlap, and the voters go to the ballot box on the backs of camels -- and the reason is clear: the process of modernization has not yet been completed; there are some roads that have been paved and others that have not yet, and there are villages that can only be reached -- by descending like revelation itself, as the narrator says -- either by parachute or by helicopter; otherwise, one must leave his homeland entirely, as if he were a hashish smuggler, to reach them via a neighboring country.

Perhaps this story illustrates what I mean: I had an Egyptian friend who was a revolutionary (he was always accusing others of having sold themselves and abandoned their revolutionary purity... etc.). Then he emigrated to the United States and completely changed his skin, working as a researcher and then a consultant at one of the strategic research centers in the United States known for its close ties to the ruling establishment. Then this friend of mine married an American girl! We do not know what happened to him, for he suffered a nervous breakdown and was consequently placed in a psychiatric facility. His wife stood by him for four years until he fully recovered, and on the day he was discharged from the hospital, she asked him for a divorce. It seems she found it her duty, by virtue of the contract between her and her husband, to stand by him until he was cured -- and this is indeed worthy of admiration -- but she also found it her right to separate from him after she had wasted this period of her life. Let us compare this incident with the following Egyptian incident: in the 1960s, obtaining a scholarship for many sons of the lower-middle class meant radical social mobility. University professors were at the top of the social ladder, and so the dream of many outstanding young people in the 1960s was to obtain a scholarship.
