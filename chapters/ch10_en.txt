The world of literature and art. My life at the university: although my life at the university constitutes my primary profession (for I did not resign from teaching until the year 2004), I nonetheless find in an intellectual autobiography such as this that I do not dwell at length on it, and it is rare among people to know that until the date of my resignation I occupied the position of Professor of Critical Theory and Nineteenth-Century English Poetry. And this is undoubtedly due to the fact that most of my publications since I obtained my doctorate revolve around the subject of Zionism. And it has other reasons as well. I cannot deny my human benefit from my experience in the Department of English Language and Literature at the Women's College, Ain Shams University. For despite the presence of a number of seconded men, I was the only male faculty member in it (and this was because I was appointed to it by mistake, for they forgot, as I mentioned, to write in the advertisement for the scholarship that it was restricted to females only). And there is no doubt that this situation increased my self-awareness and increased my ability to look at myself from the outside, and I used to say jokingly that I was the only man who receives congratulations on Mother's Day! Also, teaching at the Women's College made me understand much about women, and the dreams of equalization between men and women that had previously occurred to me no longer had any place in my vision, for I realized that woman is different from man and that equality between them does not mean equalization in any way. And I must note the general human atmosphere that prevailed in the department. For during the period I spent in it, there were no small (or large) conflicts of the kind that now prevails in the university.

We indeed think that we are no less than them in anything and that we must compete with them, and this is our slogan and our goal. But the reality is that we try to be a carbon copy of them, and so we copy from them the curricula of English literature departments and then proceed to delete some courses to make things easier for our students. But what we forget is that what corresponds to the English literature department here is not the English literature department there, but rather the Arabic literature department there — meaning that English literature for us is a foreign literature (a second literature, as they say second language), just as Arabic literature for them is a foreign literature. And this new conception requires us to exercise our minds to come up with a new conception of curricula and examinations in foreign literature departments. And the discussion was a genuine intellectual discussion, with no pedantry and no falling into academicism in the negative sense of the word. And after I taught for some time in Cairo (1974... until... 1978, 1979) I moved to Riyadh in 1979 and stayed there for six years, where I found myself in a distinguished cultural environment. King Saud University was an Arab university in the full sense of the word. Its faculty included professors from all parts of the Arab world, which gave me the opportunity to interact with this great Arab diversity. And the cultural atmosphere in Riyadh was unique. For most of the intellectuals there did not have major economic concerns. And the details of their lives were few; and we as guest professors (as we were called, contracted) had even fewer concerns and details. And given this near-total freedom, I found myself attending an innumerable number of seminars and cultural associations.

Academic literary writings: naturally, I wrote some academic studies suitable for publication in academic journals and which university professors submit to promotion committees. And since my area of specialization is English literature and Comparative Literature, they all revolve around this subject. And I made sure to marshal references in these studies, and so the committees that examined my scholarly output commended them! For example, when I applied for the position of assistant professor, the research I submitted included a study entitled The Plant and the Soil: A Comparison of the Non-Literary Backgrounds (that is, the economic, historical, and social) of Wordsworth and Whitman, and it is a passable study, but its essential characteristic is that it contains a large compilation of information. The committee that examined my work for promotion considered this study the best of what I submitted. And as one of them told me afterwards: you brought something new. And the new here is the new references and the abundant information contained in them, which I compiled. And I was careful to increase the number of references as much as possible. Indeed, I would sometimes attribute some of my own ideas to references when there was agreement between me and them, so as to create an excuse to write the title of a new reference and satisfy the appetite of the professors who evaluated my work for more documentation and new references! And this appetite is due to the illusion of receptive objectivity (to which I referred in detail earlier) and the assumption that human knowledge is cumulative and therefore the latest references that brought the latest information are the best (and this process continues until one of the foreigners says the final word!). And it seems that this disease — that is, the disease of counting the number of references as a standard of scholarship and seriousness — has gone beyond the walls of the university.

For the word initially meant pleasure (usually sexual) and carried the meaning of fleeing from pain and escaping from life (influenced in this by associationist psychology, which rests on a reductionist, mechanical vision of the human being consistent with Newton's vision of the cosmos). But gradually the word began to shed its sexual connotation and move away from the idea of escaping from life, as pleasure becomes linked with pain and with the deep feeling of human life in all its complexity (this tendency reaches its peak in Keats's Ode to Melancholy, where no one reaches joy except he who enters the temple of the goddess of sorrow, to which I referred earlier). And I showed that this transformation is part of the revolt against the Newtonian mechanical material vision and an attempt to transcend the material surface to reach human complexity. I published this study in my aforementioned book that was published in the United States. And I intend to translate the articles I wrote in English and include them in a book containing my literary studies. Studies in language: the scholar of literature must be a scholar of style and discourse and linguistic form. For literature in the end is a dense linguistic expression whose linguistic form is its meaning. And therefore we cannot arrive at a meaning separate from the words. For meaning cannot exist in the belly of the poet, and if it remained there, its knowledge would be with God or with the psychoanalyst and not with the literary critic. And I must confess that my interest in language and style, even during my literary studies, was weak, given my intense interest in thought and philosophical questions. My doctoral thesis was on a non-literary subject, although closely related to literature (the historical consciousness and the anti-historical consciousness), and I tried to shed light on it through the mechanisms of literary text analysis, and my lectures on literature were laden with philosophical reflections.

A friendship developed between me and the novelist Bahaa Taher since the 1970s, which deepened after the marriage of his daughter Dina to my son Yasser, and after we had shared grandchildren! And I came to know two American poets: the first was Jerry Stern, who had won several prizes and was a friend of Kevin Reilly; the second was an American poet of Arab Lebanese origin named Samuel Hazo (Hazou in Arabic). This poet told me an extremely amusing story worth telling, which is that in the early 1960s the fashion of buying original manuscripts of literary works began to appear, and extravagant sums were paid for them. So some famous writers resorted to writing original manuscripts for their works retroactively (that is, after their publication), and these were sold to university libraries eager to obtain such manuscripts. This is my story with literature. And it is a story that has not and will not be completed, because I have had from the beginning broad creative and critical literary ambitions. I did not write the study I had been preparing myself for on the history of modern Arabic poetry. And I had been collecting material to write a documentary novel about Raya and Sakina (I do not know the secret of my interest in them), and I had intended to go to Alexandria to consult the records of their trial, and the reason for the disagreement between them in the final moments (one broke down, but the other took a defiant position that was not remorseful about the crime and was welcoming of death). And there were many other projects, but art is long and life is short, as the Roman poet says! And with the publication of a book by Mr. Salah Issa entitled The Men of Raya and Sakina, I found a first-rate social-historical study that explains many things and presents a complex historical-social panorama before which the various characters move, and we see them as individuals and then we see them in their social context. And I found answers to all my questions, and indeed found questions I had not originally posed.

And yet my interest in the fine arts remained largely marginal until I had a sudden and profound experience at the Guggenheim Museum in New York, when I suddenly felt the entire world around me overflowing with colors, and I even heard their sounds. The Guggenheim Museum takes the shape of a funnel, and it seems I began to suffer from dizziness, and I did not recover until the guards were holding me, for I had been on the verge of falling. And what astonishes me is that the interest in color composition and architecture became from that moment a part of my vision of the world. And had I not been at that time occupied with my doctoral thesis and then the Zionist studies began tightening their grip on me, I would perhaps have changed my specialization and become an art critic. I had many artistic projects — for example, I intended to learn photography in order to visit the old villas and buildings found throughout Cairo and across all of Egypt, the well-guarded, and photograph them, and perhaps publish a book on the subject later. Also, I thought about learning naive art and practicing it because of my great love for it. But these two projects can be listed among the many projects that I will never realize! When I returned from the United States and after I had gone through the experience I referred to, my sense of the importance of architecture and the plastic arts began to deepen, considering them the art forms that the human being lives with and that shape his being and his vision at every moment without his being aware of it. And perhaps through my study of Romantic poetry I began to realize that beauty deepens belonging, unlike functionalism. For the beautiful thing assumes that the human being is a human being who does not live inside matter alone but rather lives inside it and transcends it to what lies beyond it at the same time (and from here, I link between metaphor and transcendence, indeed between metaphor and faith in God, for the materialist is confined within matter and cannot transcend it to what lies beyond it).

And the discussions were sometimes heated given the differences in tastes and aesthetic philosophy, for I tend to increase beautiful details in my home (paintings, statues, pieces of old jewelry, an old dagger... etc.), provided that the sole standard is the harmony among them. While my wife and children tend toward what I call the aesthetics of the minimum, which is the enjoyment of emptiness and silence with a minimum of ornaments and antiques. And some say that the number of pictures and heritage artifacts in my home is somewhat exaggerated, and perhaps this is a reaction to the apartment in which I grew up in Damanhour. We consulted about everything. An agreement was reached on the general outlines. And points of disagreement remained regarding the details. We were naturally constrained by the existing architectural structure, which we could not change (for that would require demolishing the building!), and from here we began to call our experience in reshaping the home transformative architecture — it is an attempt to escape from the architectural ugliness surrounding us, a functional, utilitarian architecture that treats the human being as though he were a natural creature without memory. But we cannot demolish it, for it is material wealth. And so the only thing left for us was to deal with the existing material structure and move within its limits. Then we discussed the area of the apartment, and we all agreed that the Egyptian apartment had been divided in a general manner so as to be suitable for receiving guests. And so there is an enormous, open external reception space (and this has become the latest fashion), and two small bedrooms attached to it — as though the person builds his house to move in the arena of public life, not to have a private haven in which he lives and moves. And on the basis of this realization, we agreed to abolish the idea of the salon room, for it is a wasted space that leads to the shrinking of the available living space; and naturally there was a deep-rooted hatred for the gilded salon in particular.

I also believe that Baroque music still contains the idea of the sacred (that which transcends matter), and that after it Romantic music appears with its absolute individualism, so that the individual becomes the locus of immanence.

Human knowledge is cumulative and therefore the latest references that brought the latest information are the best (and this process continues until one of the foreigners says the final word!). And it seems that this disease — that is, the disease of counting the number of references as a standard of scholarship and seriousness — has gone beyond the walls of the university. I recall that I once submitted an article to a monthly magazine about Walt Whitman — an analysis of some of his poetic texts through which I demonstrate that the best poems Whitman wrote resemble in many ways pragmatist philosophy: they are short poems that do not address any comprehensive or ultimate questions and that focus on the image or the thing directly before the poet's eyes. The magazine rejected it on the grounds that it contained no references. And I tried to explain to the editor that the article was an analysis of the texts from within, which I conducted without recourse to any reference, and that therefore my reading of the poems was entirely new. Then I told him that the article was in reality a chapter from my doctoral thesis. But to no avail. The editor was not convinced. And I was forced to publish it several years later in a magazine concerned with culture in Lebanon. And yet my academic studies expressed some of my intellectual concerns (as happened in my doctoral thesis). I wrote a study entitled The Transcendental Predicament, in which I studied the model of self-centeredness that leads to object-centeredness (one of the most important features of the comprehensive secular model) in the writings of Emerson, Thoreau, and other writers of the Transcendentalist movement. And I argued in this study that the source of this model is the search for an absolute freedom of the self — a freedom impossible to achieve that leads to the exact opposite, for it is a freedom that devours itself.

And in a study of Ibsen's play Rosmersholm, I studied the model of transition from innocence to experience, or from simplification and reductionism to complexity, and this is what I did in several other studies. I also wrote a study entitled The Dialectic of Man and Nature in Thoreau's Book Walden, in which I showed that Thoreau escapes from the model of oscillation between self-centeredness and object-centeredness and arrives at a compound dialectical model that neither surrenders to nature nor tries to conquer it, but rather tries to achieve balance with it. And I developed the concept of gentle conflict (in the Islamic lexicon, tadafu' — and this is a term that was not yet part of my lexicon) where we find that the human being is not merely a part of nature, nor its conqueror, but rather its master — kind, merciful, deriving the elements of his survival from it, yet maintaining a relationship of harmony with it.
