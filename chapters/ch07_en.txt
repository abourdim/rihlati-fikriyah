Receptive objectivity and the university: I discovered that much of what I had thought were pathological academic phenomena was the result of this receptive stance toward reality. When I returned from the United States in 1974, the head of the department (at the Ain Shams Women's College) instructed me that my lectures should contain no fewer than ten pieces of information that I would transmit to the students, who were expected to receive this information and thereby increase their knowledge. Then he added that if I accomplished this matter of the ten, that would satisfy him completely. The department head wanted to interfere in my lectures to make sure that I was giving the students those same ten pieces of information, so I decided to keep him away from my domain and my teaching method, which was my right. But instead of confrontation, I used the informational weapon with cunning and shrewdness, for I told him that I was giving the students a historical background before addressing Romantic critical theory itself, and therefore I would be teaching them the critic Lowth. And this Lowth is a critic of no importance whatsoever, and no one has heard of him, for this reason. But instead of the department head arguing with me about the importance of this critic and the benefit of teaching his theories to my students at the women's college, he fell silent. Because he was surprised by a piece of information he had not heard before and did not dare to ask about its value or importance — for such questions are subjective, having no objective receptive basis! And receptive objectivity means in reality submission and capitulation before material reality and the rejection of the idea that the mind arranges and formulates the sensory data that reach it and that the writer has the right to indeed reformulate reality to convey his vision. And perhaps one of the most amusing examples of this was what I heard in a literary criticism lecture given by a famous female professor.

And the disease of receptive objectivity has reached — as expected — the criteria by which professors are promoted. When I began preparing my research for promotion, I asked one of the members of the promotion committee about the criteria for promotion, and he said: to bring new information. The professor, the committee member, also emphasized the importance of references and the necessity that I consult the latest ones. So in my research submitted for promotion, I made sure to present my point of view, then I adorned it with the latest references so as to calm the nerves of whoever would evaluate my work.

And I always advise them, instead of that, that research should be through topics and problematics (like this journey). And to clarify the idea of more interpretive and less interpretive, I would give them an amusing example from when I was in one of the European capitals. I noticed that the girls were extremely thin and were wearing extremely short skirts. So I would tell the students that one could say that poverty had swept Europe, and that the girls were thin because they were not eating enough, and the skirts were short because they clearly could not afford long skirts. This interpretive model explains some aspects of reality without doubt. But what about the exorbitantly expensive watch that the thin girl is wearing?! And what about the shoes and the handbag? Here this interpretive model collapses because it is less interpretive. And we propose another model, the fashion model, which imposes on girls that they be thin and wear short skirts. And we will find that this latter model is capable of explaining a greater number of elements. And so it is more interpretive. And transcending receptive objectivity and direct observation was my constant practice in my studies and research, including my studies in Zionism. I have mentioned before how I interpreted Jewish immigration figures. And I can mention here another incident. It is the construction of the Holocaust Museum in the United States. At the time, some people said that this was an expression of the power of Zionist influence... etc. But after a little research and scrutiny, I discovered that the Zionist state was not entirely happy with this museum. For it considers itself the center of Jews and Judaism, and the Holocaust had been transformed into a fundamental landmark of what is called Zionist history, and they had established the Yad Vashem memorial in Israel to serve as a shrine where the people worship their history and themselves — it is like a sacred place, indeed it is the most sacred of places.

And among the most important things I learned from him was taking Jewish and Zionist phenomena out of the circle of the Torah, the Talmud, and Jewish studies, and placing them within the scope of general human knowledge; and putting them in several historical contexts so that they become different phenomena with different dimensions, and not a single, slippery phenomenon characterized by unity. But the most important thing I learned from him — and this is what I learned from my teachers (such as Dr. Emil George, Dr. Nour Sherif, Dr. David Weimer) — was the method of thinking and looking and how to contemplate information and interpret it. I learned from Gamal Hamdan how patterns are discovered within the rubble of changing details and how one strips truth from facts. And I do not know whether I also learned from him something of resilience and the capacity for resistance? The influence of Gamal Hamdan cannot be found in a line or two or a page or two of his writings but rather is there between the lines, and this is the deepest kind of influence. But with the dominance of the cumulative informational model, the importance of this kind of influence has been neglected. The field of scholarly research for many is about facts and not about truth, about information and not about the patterns behind them. And so when the influence of one writer on another is studied, scholars usually search for a few sentences, phrases, and direct quotations that the influenced writer took from the influential writer... And the list of references in studies written within this informational model means that the contributions of dozens of thinkers and teachers to shaping the ideas of scholars are unrecognized because they do not exist from a quantitative informational perspective. I could also raise another question: why did Gamal Hamdan not influence those who write studies on the same subject in a way commensurate with his intellectual stature? I can say that the cumulative informational model has completely dominated and transformed everything (opinions, visions, dreams, and pains) into information.

As it was also emphasized that all matters are relative — meaning equal — and that there is no standard for judgment, which means undermining faith in anything, indeed undermining the self so that it becomes devoid of purpose and goal and any form of ideals that transcend the narrow self. So sending such a person to a foreign country based on a vision related to foreign policy that he does not understand and does not care about, and for motives that have been concealed from him — and he is in any case centered on himself, seeking what satisfies his consumerist appetite — he will not fight fiercely and will not endure any pain; for the human being only endures pain through his belief in something that transcends his narrow self. And when I returned from the United States in 1974, I went to give my first lecture to the students (male and female) at the Faculty of Arts at Ain Shams University (as I had been seconded there). And I was told that the lecture was in a certain auditorium, so I went to that auditorium and entered, and found that there was a large number of girls sitting in the front who had put on a great deal of makeup and were wearing colorful dresses. So I went out immediately, thinking that there was a party and that I had come to the wrong place. For my American and Egyptian cognitive models (at least until the beginning of the seventies) were the lens through which I saw; and according to these models, girls do not put on such cosmetics and do not wear such dresses except at parties (as was the case at the University of Alexandria when I left it, and at the American universities where I studied). But one of the students hurried out of the auditorium to inform me that this was not a party but rather a lecture, and I had to adjust my cognitive model. For I realized that the difference between a party and a lecture was no longer as great as it had been in the past! And yet there is a conscious deployment of cognitive models, as in television commercials, when the advertisement director realizes that all the noble and base instincts of the human being can be deployed to market the advertised product, so he links, for example, one brand of cooking fat with marital happiness, and a type of soft drink or perfume with sexual attractiveness, and the emotion of fatherhood with the mobile and non-mobile telephone, and so on. And challenging the dominant cognitive model may lead to negative feelings, for it exposes us before ourselves and adjusts our map, which is no easy thing. I participated in a seminar at the Haus der Kulturen der Welt in Berlin, attended by Dr. Nasr Hamid Abu Zayd, Dr. Ridwan al-Sayyid, Dr. Arkoun, and others.

The human being discovers a relationship between two things or two phenomena that no one had discovered before and links them together, then abstracts, after this linking process, a general pattern (an interpretive model) that transcends the two phenomena and has a reasonable explanatory power, then one sees reality anew in the light of this new relationship. And the process of linking is a subjective act, because it is the product of the exercise of thought, and not a material given that exists ready-made in reality. And the process of abstraction is a creative process, even more subjective than the process of linking. And I always emphasize to my students that the processes of linking and abstraction cannot take place in a vacuum nor from within the text or the phenomenon alone. Rather, the scholar must be highly cultured so that he can discover the recurring pattern, for if the perceiving self is not rich, capable of absorbing the complexity of reality, it will fall into self-enclosure and passivity. For all of this, I found it more beneficial to discard the terms objective and subjective (for they presuppose an object existing in itself and an independent, isolated subject that does not engage with the object). And I replaced them with the terms more interpretive and less interpretive, for they are more precise in describing the process of perception and interpretation. If the thesis that the scholar presents explains a number of data exceeding the number explained by prevailing theses, then it is more interpretive; and if its number is less, then it is less interpretive. And these two terms are distinguished by the fact that they do not ignore reality in an excessively subjective manner, while at the same time they affirm the importance of the mind and its capacity to interact with the object and link different data. The two new terms are also more open. For the human being presents his thesis to be tested against the touchstone of reality, not to be accepted or rejected, and after testing it, if he finds it more interpretive he takes it up, and perhaps adds to it to make its interpretive power higher; but if it is less interpretive, then he points to its deficiencies and completes it.

The belief that the human sciences do not fundamentally differ from the natural sciences, because the human phenomenon in its essence does not differ from the natural material phenomenon. And the study of influence according to this receptive objectivist method takes the form of searching for specific images, phrases, or words (and sometimes even ideas) that the influenced writer took from the influential writer. And the researcher must show in an objective manner the actual and material channels through which the influence was transmitted. And whoever undertakes a study of influence in this framework must produce the material, objective, tangible evidence for the validity of his thesis and must transform himself from a literary analyst into a police detective! And I had begun my scholarly life with a study of this kind, for I spent — as I mentioned — three years writing a master's thesis entitled The Influence of English Romantic Poetry and French Symbolist Poetry (especially Charles Baudelaire) on the Poetry of Ibrahim Naji. And the matter was supposed to be extremely simple because the poet Ibrahim Naji had translated the collection Les Fleurs du mal into Arabic (from English). But when I began the study, I found that the influence existed and in abundance, but it was trivial and superficial, mere verbal echoes that did not change the poet's sensibility or his vision. Indeed, I found that Naji's distortion of Baudelaire and his failure to understand the French poet (because of his intellectual and literary heritage) were more important than those moments in which he was directly influenced by him.
