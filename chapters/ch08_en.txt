The Encyclopedia — it is necessary to clarify an important point. And that is that my interest in politics was primarily an epistemological, philosophical interest, and that my interest in daily political events remained secondary and marginal, ignoring newspapers and collective hysteria! For example, I was in the United States in 1967 when the Naksa (the setback) occurred, and the American media celebrated the Israeli victory with hysterical fanfare, and yet I began my doctoral thesis immediately after the war, ignoring the daily newspapers and the television and the media hysteria. Then the war of 1973 broke out and I was busy writing the 1976 encyclopedia. And my wife — like most Egyptians — was glued to the television. And I continued my work without stopping. But I asked my wife to tell me when she saw some Israeli prisoners so that I could see them with my own eyes. And this was for me a genuine experience. I, who claim to observe the events of the present as a historian. And yet I must mention a scene I shall never forget that was broadcast by American television right after the 1967 war. Moshe Dayan was delivering a speech to some Egyptian prisoners being returned to Egypt, and the topic of his speech was of course peace (for the Israelis — as their behavior shows — ask for nothing but peace and prosperity for all!). In any case, Dayan told the returning soldiers to convey to the Egyptian leadership their sincere Zionist desire for peace. The soldiers did not respond to him, and their faces took on a silence and a form of determination whose meaning Dayan understood. And when the soldiers boarded the bus, they chanted: Nasser — Nasser. And the commentator said: it is clear that the soldiers will not convey this peace message to the Egyptian leadership. And this does not mean that I do not participate in daily political work, for I have my participations and contributions.

And I recall that I mentioned to Mr. Heikal that the employees at the ministry were confused about my exact position and my precise place (and this is determined, of course, by the extent of my proximity to, or distance from, the Minister). Mr. Heikal understood my situation, so he would invite me to his office once a week for discussions and conversations, and I would stay in the ministry the rest of the week! And I was studying for my master's degree in sociology at the American University, so he decided to attend one of the courses with me, and it was on the history of Egypt (and the news agencies circulated the story and tried to interpret it in a deep strategic manner!). And my relationship with Mr. Heikal was defined from the beginning until now as a deep intellectual and personal relationship that transcends political considerations. And since I came to know Mr. Heikal, he has been generous enough to give me much of his time; he would read most of what I wrote and discuss it with me, being enthusiastic about some of it and reserved about the rest. I remember that I wrote a group of articles about the civilizational situation in the United States (which were collected in the book The Earthly Paradise). He read them and expressed his admiration, then said: and yet I will take an opposing position. And he began to present the opposing point of view and engaged me in a manner that exhausted me greatly, for he was able to show the strengths of the opposing thesis and the weaknesses of what I was proposing (and perhaps his ability to debate with me on this subject was due to his own doubts, as an Arab nationalist, regarding Western modernity that is detached from value and historical memory and that does not recognize national particularities and that has ended with a Western globalization wishing to sweep the world). And I do not think that I would have found anyone who would agree to publish a study entitled Shaul Tchernichovsky and the Metaphysics of Secular Zionism, or an article entitled The New Zion in the United States, except Mr. Heikal.

For the government knew that the Egyptian armed forces had performed well during the War of Attrition, but it also knew that exhaustion had taken its toll on them, and what was needed was for them to catch their breath. Also, the Egyptian leadership wanted to move the missiles to the shore of the Canal to protect the Egyptian forces (in preparation for the crossing). And it was the view of the Egyptian leadership that the Palestine Liberation Organization should move as it wished, provided that it did not attack Egypt. For Egypt is a state, while the Organization is a guerrilla movement, and each has its own limits and independent dynamics. And I found that the Palestinian leader largely agreed with my view, but he added that he could not announce this because he could not control the camps, as it seemed that the camp residents had been incited in an irrational manner that made it impossible to direct them rationally. And I was stunned by his response, and then what happened happened — the attack on Egypt, and Black September and the massacres that no one wants to mention or remember. And at roughly the same time this incident occurred. It seems that the political leadership in Egypt at the time found itself largely isolated from public opinion and knew nothing about it. So Mr. Heikal asked the advisors to do something... and we discovered that there was something called internal information, and among its tasks was that the responsible employee would write a report on public opinion (and so this employee was called the public opinion officer); and it was supposed that the sum of these reports would give the government a reasonable idea of the pulse of the street. But what happened was the opposite. For the public opinion officer would receive his instructions from the Governor, who would ask him to write rosy reports. And this situation repeated itself until it became the rule and not the exception.

For example, he would tell us what to expect in terms of drops in temperature, and he said: By God, by God, it is really cold — then he kept repeating the sentence and piling on the oaths, and this was perhaps his notion of the Egyptian colloquial. And it seems that he learned the Egyptian colloquial when his parents were working at one of the missionary stations in Assiut, where there is a large Coptic community. (And not many people know that the Protestant missionary campaigns were directed primarily at the Copts of Egypt so that they would leave their national church.) After the customary protocol greetings with the Ambassador, I said to him: the United States is trying to take a fair position on the Palestinian question, and this is something to be commended. However, it is impossible because Israel cannot survive without American support. And the very survival of Israel is an injustice to the Palestinians because it means their displacement and the entrenchment of the theft of their homeland. Then I asked him: if matters crystallized in the Arab world and reached the degree of polarization such that the United States had to choose between the Zionist state and the Arab states, what would happen then? Would the United States choose the Arab side or the Zionist side? And the question was somewhat naive, but it was a hypothetical question that could shed light on an important issue. And his response was telling in the extreme. He said: the United States prefers to have Arab policies equal in number to the Arab states (meaning that it prefers not to take a crystallized position and favors the state of fragmentation in the Arab world so that it can issue balanced statements, without taking any actual measures, of course). And the years passed and matters remained as they were. For in 1977...

Then we talked about the Frankfurt School and I told him that in my view it was the best critique of comprehensive secularism and relativism from within the system. He pointed to Karl Mannheim and asked: can he too be classified in the same way? And we talked after that about Max Weber and the problematic of the origins of capitalism. In short, the conversation was varied and deep, indicative of a first-rate intellectual mind, and I believe his country lost a great deal by dismissing him and defaming him. And among the anecdotes I must mention is that in my boyhood a friendship developed between me and a boy from the Maldives (Maldives now) who was studying at al-Azhar, named Maumoon Abdul Gayoom. And the bonds of friendship between us strengthened, so he would visit me in Damanhour and I would visit him in Cairo, and we exchanged letters for some time. Until the correspondence between us stopped, perhaps because of the postal service. And once I was sitting in front of the television in Saudi Arabia and it was announced that the President of the Maldives had arrived, and when he came down from the plane it was none other than my old friend, so we renewed the relationship. And he invited my wife and me to visit his beautiful country, and we spent a few days on its enchanting islands, and I gave a lecture on Islam and modernity, and we spent some time with him and members of his family. My relationship with Zionism: while my intellectual vision and my analytical models were taking shape, Zionism had begun to transform into the primary intellectual and political preoccupation of my life. And perhaps it is time for me to address how I deal with it and my relationship to it. And the starting point here is not at all contentious, but rather quite specific. When I was a child in Damanhour, we used to hear about the moulid of Sidi Abu Hasira (the Jewish saint) in a neighboring village. And we would sometimes go to attend that moulid, which did not differ much from any other moulid. I do not remember any of its details, although I do not recall any problems being raised at that time. And sitting next to me on the bench was Maurice Daoud Maleh.

But the days turn and the Zionist state proceeds to acknowledge its involvement, and even grants the rank of Major in the Israeli army to the name of Dr. Marzouk after the Egyptian authorities executed him. It also gave him and Azar the name Kedoshei Kahir (meaning the Martyrs of Cairo). The point in all of this is that Khawaga Cohen did not emigrate to Israel but rather to Australia, where he still lives, according to the latest news we have about him! And the tears of Khawaga Cohen remained merely question marks in my imagination, searching for an answer. And it can be said that my real relationship with Zionism began in 1957 when I went to Columbia University in New York to obtain a master's degree in English and Comparative Literature. I had at that time a set of firm convictions, among them that Israel (which one was not allowed to refer to except by adding the word "alleged") was a country inhabited by Zionist gangs that the Arab forces could eliminate at any moment they decided to do so. And for this reason, I decided to ignore the subject entirely, because if the matter was trivial to this extent, why should I bother myself with it? Why should we halt Arab history because of something alleged, unreal, that we can uproot completely and eliminate whenever we decide? And the Palestinian question was presented as a refugee question — people expelled from their homes who must be given justice. And so the solution was simply to return some of them to their homes (especially since Israel was at the time declaring that it did not object to that) and to settle the rest in the Arab homeland. Then the Palestinian workers and peasants would ally with the Israeli workers and peasants to fight class exploitation and to overthrow all exploitative systems in the region (making no distinction between the Arab systems and the Zionist system) and we would establish a society with no place for classes or exploitation.

And how he was forced to flee with his mother, and how the Zionist bullets would strike their feet so that they would flee far from their homes, leaving them for the settler-colonial Zionists. And the Zionist lies that the Western media repeated increased his pain and bitterness. And the American media kept affirming a statement falsely attributed to President Abdel Nasser, which was his demand to throw Israel into the sea. It also claimed that Jews were prevented from visiting the Jewish sacred sites in Jordan (the Wailing Wall, meaning the Buraq Wall in Islamic terminology). We would challenge them to prove the occasion on which Abdel Nasser uttered the statement referred to. We would also offer to have a journalist visit the Wailing Wall in Jordan himself. And we would explain to them that the issue was that the Arabs did not recognize Israel and therefore no person could visit Israel and then the holy sites in Jordan; rather, he had to visit Jordan on its own. We would bring them the documents that demolished their media myths from their foundations, but the matter would be completely ignored as if nothing had happened, and then they would continue promoting the rumors and repeating the claims. And here I began to discover — as I mentioned before — that the West's support for Israel was because it was a settler enclave serving its interests, just like the other settler enclaves, and that it was an expression of a larger, deep-rooted pattern in the Western consciousness, which I referred to earlier as the complete faith in pragmatism that rests on a fierce Darwinian foundation, and that the issue of power alone, and the iron material fist, are myths with no basis in history or reality.

The family was completely assimilated into American society — its dreams American, its furniture American, its language American. In any case, American society made the process of assimilation as easy as possible. Then Thelma told me about her experience in Israel and confided to me that she harbored a deep hatred for the Zionist state. She fell like... and so on, and she ended up enjoying the nature and the antiquities and the company of the kibbutz youth. Then she discovered that most of the youth of the kibbutz... the Zionist state, at the first opportunity, to the American Promised Land. Then she confessed to me that when she had told me she was Jewish with that aggressiveness, she was covering up her feeling of guilt because of her disgust with Zion. As for her sister Sandra, she was more forthcoming, for she admitted that she went to Israel in search of a bridegroom! (And Sandra eventually succeeded in finding a bridegroom in New Jersey; he was a tall, broad, blond, non-Jewish young man. Her mother wept on the wedding day. But she accepted the fait accompli and would often show me her grandson, carrying him with intense passion.) And after the marriage, Sandra became completely indifferent to the Zionist state, but she would give generously to the Jewish fundraising agency, which would assure her (and other American Jews) that the money was spent on the elderly and orphans and on museums and the arts, not on settlements and shells. And she would pay what she paid because she had completely stopped practicing any Jewish religious rituals including dietary laws.

The analytical model that I developed for Jewish and Zionist phenomena in a manner that I believe is complex to a considerable degree and does not fall into reductionism. And my relationship with Thelma Schenkel (my colleague at Columbia University who told me she was Jewish, not American) deepened, and she introduced my wife and me to her family (her parents and siblings) in Fort Lee, New Jersey. We were surprised that Thelma the Jewess was constantly mocking Jews and her parents (because of their Jewish habits and their Yiddish accent), and she would even mock the furniture of her home, seeing it as the epitome of vulgarity (not very different from the furniture of newly wealthy middle-class Egyptian homes), and she would refer to it as Renaissance Juive style, meaning Jewish Renaissance. And an intimate relationship developed between me and the mother, who had been living in one of the small Polish towns before her immigration to the United States, and it seems she had never heard of the Arab-Israeli conflict. For this reason, she would ask my wife and me to find a bridegroom for her daughter (a nice Jewish young man to marry her), and we would smile and promise her good things. And while the old generation was doing its utmost to preserve the remnants of its Eastern European Slavic culture (which they called Jewish), the new generation was trying (its utmost too) to rid itself of it with all its might and at the earliest time and at the first opportunity. The family was completely assimilated into American society — its dreams American, its furniture American, its language American. In any case, American society made the process of assimilation as easy as possible. The Arab identity rings out singing in classical Arabic on his land despite the existence of a powerful, armed settler-colonial state that does its utmost to erase him and erase his history and to deny his existence. The Palestinian human being, through the poetry of Darwish, became for me all of humanity; and the Palestinian struggle became the symbol of the human being in a materialist reality that knows nothing but rational adaptation. Specializing in Zionism: all the previous elements contributed to my decision to specialize in Zionism. And I wrote to the Egyptian cultural attache with the innocence and enthusiasm of youth, requesting that he convert my scholarship from the study of English literature to the study of Hebrew and politics. And the man realized at that moment that he was dealing with a madman, so he called me on the telephone and told me, in effect: stop this nonsense — that is, stop the madness, and finish your studies. For changing the subject of a scholarship is a matter that requires the mobilization of the entire Egyptian state, and perhaps the President of the Republic himself is unable to accomplish it, for the laws constrain everyone. So I decided to comply with the orders, and the man moreover considered that people like me who specialize in Zionism and ideology were wasting their time on theoretical matters that were, in his view, mere scholarly ornamentation. The Arabs could boast of the rigorous scholarly studies written by Arab scholars on this subject. But they are not very useful in making political and military decisions (for he was a dyed-in-the-wool bureaucrat who believed that the government knew everything and took all necessary measures). Despite this negative position, I decided to specialize in Zionism. And gradually, English and American and Comparative Literature (my academic specialization) became marginal. And as I indicated before, my doctoral thesis was the arena in which I developed the analytical models that I used in studying Zionist and Jewish phenomena.

And there were other Jewish figures in our lives (like Khawaga Dasa, owner of a small textile factory in Manshiyya that my father bought, or Khawaga Hamburger, owner of the Lion Textile Factory that my father also bought). But all of these remained marginal or ordinary figures who raised no problematics — they were merely khawagat or foreigners (like many of Egypt's Jews), no different from other foreign capitalists residing in Egypt, who departed with the arrival of Abdel Nasser to power and the adoption of economic and political Egyptianization policies.
